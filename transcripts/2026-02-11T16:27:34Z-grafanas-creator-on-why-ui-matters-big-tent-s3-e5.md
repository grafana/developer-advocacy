## Summary

In this podcast discussion, the hosts delve into the aesthetic aspects of user interface design, particularly in the context of the software Grafana, which has recently celebrated its 12th release. The conversation features insights from Toqual, who emphasizes the importance of creating visually appealing software, noting that a well-designed interface can enhance user experience and engagement. They reflect on the evolution of Grafana's design, acknowledging that the initial versions already exhibited a strong focus on aesthetics. The hosts also discuss the broader implications of design in software, highlighting that a beautiful interface can differentiate similar products and contribute to their success. The conversation reveals a shared appreciation for the intersection of functionality and design in the tech industry.

**Was that intentional when you built this?** 

You didn't need to make it look good, right? It could have just been black lines or something on white paper. 

> **You clearly don't know Toqual.** [laughter]

Well, it was intentional in the sense that this was maybe the third kind of UI project that I built at that point. For me personally, I like working with software that looks good. That's just a preference of mine. I know that I'm going to invest more time in it and enjoy using it more if it looks cool and is well-designed. So, that was mostly just working towards my own preference. If I'm happy working on it and using it, maybe others will be too.

> **Yeah, I agree with that.** 

I think people still underestimate that. The whole Web 2.0 thing showed us that websites can actually look pleasant, be usable, and easy to navigate. That’s when I really started to pay attention to front-end design. You can have two bits of software that are identical, but the one with the more beautiful interface feels like it’s going to win. We’re still humans, and it still matters. So, I agree with you, and I always appreciate it. 

I saw some pictures of early Grafana, and it looked great from the beginning. I think, why not? 

Now, because this podcast is about the 12th release of Grafana, can you believe it's been 12 releases now? Did you ever think we would still be working on this project 12 years later? 

> **No. Far from it.** 

Before I started Grafana, I think the longest I had worked at a single company or on a single project was maybe two and a half years. So, starting something that I would be working on 12 years later was very far from my expectations.
